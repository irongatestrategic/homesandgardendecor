## What to Look for in an Outdoor Side Table

HDPE poly-lumber is the correct material choice for zero-maintenance outdoor side tables at this price point. It's non-porous, doesn't absorb moisture, and won't rot or support mold growth. Color is integral to the material rather than surface-applied, which means UV exposure doesn't produce peeling or flaking — the finish degrades as slowly as the material itself, which is measured in decades rather than seasons.

Hardware is where budget furniture most often fails. Standard steel fasteners corrode in coastal or high-humidity environments, producing rust streaks on the table surface and structural loosening at the joints. Zinc-plated hardware offers marginal improvement but will still oxidize within two to three seasons of salt-air exposure. Stainless steel — specifically 304-grade — is the only specification that matches the long-term performance of poly-lumber boards.

Weight matters for function in a side table. A table under 10 lbs displaces in wind, which means it's regularly knocked over or blown off a porch. An HDPE table in the 15 to 20 lb range stays where you put it without anchoring.

Height and surface area need to match the seating they'll serve. Standard garden chairs with a seat height of 17 to 19 inches pair correctly with a side table surface at 18 to 20 inches — within 4 inches of arm height. Adirondack-style chairs sit lower at 14 to 16 inches and need a correspondingly lower table. A 12x12 inch surface handles a single drink. For genuine utility — book, phone, and a drink — 18x18 inches is the practical minimum. Above 24x24 inches you're into bistro table territory, which starts consuming floor space in small configurations.

## What to Avoid in Outdoor Side Tables Under $200

Painted MDF or particleboard: compressed wood fiber held with adhesive. Once the surface paint chips, moisture enters the core and the material swells and disintegrates within a single wet season. Not a side table — a liability.

Zinc hardware at connection points: corrodes within two to three seasons in humid or coastal environments. Produces rust staining on the table surface and structural loosening at the joints. Only 304-grade stainless is appropriate for long-term outdoor hardware.

Resin tops on thin steel frames: the steel corrodes at the bracket attachment points, and thin resin tops crack in freeze-thaw cycles. The combination of two weak material choices doesn't add up to one durable product.

Short warranties on any side table: a 2-year warranty on outdoor furniture signals that the manufacturer expects the product to fail or degrade significantly within that window. Anything intended as a permanent outdoor fixture should carry a minimum 10-year warranty, and HDPE-based products should carry 20 years.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What height should an outdoor side table be?**
For standard garden chairs, 18 to 22 inches puts the surface within comfortable reach of the chair arm without requiring a reach down or up. The specific target is within 4 inches of the chair arm height — measure your chairs before ordering a standalone table.

**What is the most weather-resistant material for an outdoor table?**
HDPE poly-lumber is the strongest case for zero-maintenance weather resistance. It doesn't absorb moisture, won't rot, and carries UV resistance through the full material thickness rather than as a surface coating. Powder-coated aluminum is a close second but requires touch-up if the coating chips. Teak is durable but needs annual oiling to prevent silvering.

**Can you leave an outdoor side table out in winter?**
HDPE poly-lumber tables can stay outside year-round. The material doesn't absorb moisture, so freeze-thaw cycles have no structural effect. Tables with MDF, particleboard, or thin resin tops should be stored — freeze-thaw cycling destroys those materials from the inside out.
